Financial QA · System Design

The system that makes sure
nothing leaves unreconciled.

Built to catch financial discrepancies before they reach clients. Not a spreadsheet — a gate. Every invoice is checked against its committed order amount before it moves.

⚠

The Problem

Client-facing invoices were leaving without systematic reconciliation. An undetected mismatch reaching a client was a real, present risk.

⚙

The System

Excel tracker with automated error-flagging. When invoice amount ≠ committed MCO amount, an alert triggers — before anything is sent.

✓

The Result

5–10 discrepancies caught every month. Zero unreconciled invoices have reached a client since the system went live.

Context
The environment and what was already in place

MediaMint is a growth-stage media investment firm operating at the intersection of brand growth and equity-aligned media partnerships — placing portfolio companies across premium TV, streaming, digital, and OOH inventory. The operation involves multiple simultaneous stakeholders: portfolio companies including Survey Corps Media, Straw Hat Ventures, Fairy Guild Co., Flame Hashira Group, and Black Bull Capital — alongside media partners, internal analytics, finance, and campaign teams.

When I joined, the firm was in an early operational stage. Campaigns were live, clients were active, deliverables were going out — but the infrastructure underneath was largely absent. The operation was running on individual knowledge, verbal updates, and manual coordination.

No centralised task tracking. No structured documentation. No QA system. No reliable way to know at any given moment what was committed, what was delivered, and what was at risk.
2
The Problem
Three recurring failure modes that needed to be solved
Failure Mode 1 — Tasks Falling Off

Without centralised tracking, work was managed through individual memory and verbal updates. A critical deliverable was nearly missed — not through negligence, but because there was no system to catch what fell through the cracks. The near-miss made it clear the operation could not continue running on informal coordination.

Failure Mode 2 — No Data Integrity Gate

Client-facing documents — invoices, campaign reports, performance decks — were being prepared and sent without a structured review process. Financial figures were not systematically reconciled before documents left the building. The risk of an undetected error reaching a client was real and present.

Failure Mode 3 — No Institutional Knowledge

Everything lived in individual heads. New team members had no structured way to learn the operation. No playbooks, no reference documents, no onboarding framework. Every new person started from scratch and required intensive hand-holding through tasks that should have been self-service.

3
My Role
What I was responsible for — and what I took on beyond the brief

I was the sole builder of every system described in this case study. No operational infrastructure was assigned to me — I identified the gaps, designed the solutions, and built and deployed each system while simultaneously running day-to-day campaign operations across a multi-million dollar portfolio.

Cross-functional coordination

Delivery required simultaneous coordination across four layers — client stakeholders (AVP and Finance Head) for escalations and approvals; an internal operations team of two associates for task execution, which I assigned and managed directly; an account lead for escalation routing; and externally, 13 media partners and 12 portfolio companies for creative delivery and campaign reporting. I was the consistent point of contact across all four layers, managing information flow, escalation routing, and delivery accountability without a formal PM title.

What I Learned
Four honest observations from building from scratch
01
Infrastructure is invisible until it's absent
The operation ran for months without systems. Nobody noticed what was missing until something nearly fell through. Building proactively — before the failure — is the hardest part of operational work to justify and the most valuable thing to do.
02
Build for replacement, not indispensability
The instinct in most organisations is to make yourself hard to replace. The better instinct is to build systems so well-documented that your absence doesn't create a crisis. That's what good operational infrastructure actually means.
03
AI augmentation multiplies consistency, not just speed
Using AI to build communication templates, briefing systems, and documentation didn't just save time — it raised the consistency floor across every output. AI-augmented work is more consistent than manually produced work at the same volume.
04
Training is the final layer of operations
A system that only one person can run is not a system. Getting team members to independently execute against operational standards is what makes infrastructure durable. Training wasn't separate from the build — it was the last step of it.
